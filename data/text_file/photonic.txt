Paper 2 Review — Miller (2017), "Attojoule optoelectronics for communications and computing"
Miller (2017) provides a foundational analysis of energy consumption in modern computing systems, arguing that interconnects—not logic—are now the dominant bottleneck due to the non-scaling nature of electrical wire charging and associated communication overheads. The paper demonstrates that while logic operations have become extremely energy-efficient, data movement still consumes orders of magnitude more energy, primarily because of capacitive losses and additional circuit requirements such as amplification and clock recovery. It proposes optical interconnects as the only scalable solution, highlighting their ability to eliminate wire charging, reduce or remove auxiliary electronic circuitry, and enable significantly higher bandwidth density and signal integrity. Furthermore, the work outlines the path toward achieving attojoule-level energy per bit through tightly integrated, low-capacitance optoelectronic devices, while also acknowledging practical challenges in device scaling and system integration. Paper 3 Review — Hughes et al. (2018), "Training of photonic neural networks through in situ backpropagation"
This paper shows how to train photonic neural networks directly on the hardware using a method called TRIM (Time-Reversal Interference Method). Instead of relying on slow simulations or inefficient one-by-one parameter tweaks, it uses the physics of light itself to compute gradients: by sending signals forward and backward through the chip, interfering them, and measuring light intensities, the system can figure out how to update all parameters at once. This approach works in situ (on the chip), scales efficiently, and remains robust to real-world imperfections. The authors demonstrate that it can successfully train small photonic networks for tasks like XOR and simple classification, making it a practical breakthrough similar to how backpropagation enabled deep learning in traditional computing.
Paper 4 review— Clements et al. (2016), "Optimal design for universal multiport interferometers"
The Clements et al. 2017 optimal multiport interferometer design paper proposes a more efficient design for photonic chips used in neural networks by improving how beam splitters are arranged in multiport interferometers. It replaces the older Reck triangular layout, which suffers from large size and uneven optical loss, with a symmetric rectangular mesh that keeps the same number of components but reduces optical depth from (2N-3) to (N), cuts the physical footprint roughly in half, and significantly improves loss tolerance by balancing path lengths. The authors also introduce a matrix decomposition algorithm that systematically maps any unitary transformation to specific beam splitter settings, proving the design is universal and practical. Overall, the Clements design is a more compact, robust, and scalable architecture, which is why it has become the standard in photonic machine learning systems.

Paper 6 — Saade et al. (2016), "Random projections through multiple layers of a linear optical network"
The Saade et al. 2015 random projections optical scattering paper ցույցs that the heavy computation required for kernel-based machine learning can be offloaded to physics by using light scattering through a disordered medium as a natural random projector. Instead of explicitly generating and multiplying by a large random matrix, input data is encoded onto a laser via a digital micromirror device, passed through a scattering material (like white paint), and the resulting speckle pattern is captured by a camera, effectively performing a fast random linear transformation followed by a nonlinear intensity operation. The authors show that this process approximates an elliptic kernel, enabling efficient classification without storing or computing large matrices, and demonstrate near-theoretical performance on MNIST (~2% error vs. 1.31% ideal), far outperforming linear baselines. Unlike programmable photonic interferometers, this approach sacrifices control for simplicity and extreme speed, leveraging inherent physical randomness as a computational resource.
Paper 7 — Hamerly et al. (2019), "Large-scale optical neural networks based on photoelectric multiplication"
The Hamerly et al. 2019 large scale optical neural networks photoelectric multiplication paper introduces a fundamentally different approach to optical neural networks that avoids the scalability limits of chip-based interferometer meshes by encoding both inputs and weights as optical signals and performing multiplication through homodyne detection, where light interference directly produces multiply-accumulate operations. Using free-space optics and large detector arrays, the system can scale to over a million neurons and be reprogrammed at GHz speeds, while also supporting both inference and training within the same hardware. The authors further identify a Standard Quantum Limit (SQL) set by shot noise, establishing a minimum energy per operation that can be far lower than conventional digital systems, potentially reaching orders-of-magnitude better efficiency than GPUs and ASICs. Overall, this architecture trades compactness for massive scalability and speed, positioning it as a powerful alternative to both structured photonic chips and random scattering approaches.
Paper 8 — Bandyopadhyay et al. (2022), "Single chip photonic deep neural network with backward propagation"
The Bandyopadhyay et al. 2022 FICONN photonic neural network paper presents the first fully integrated photonic deep neural network on a single chip, combining linear layers, nonlinear activations, and in-situ training into one system . It introduces key innovations including an energy-efficient on-chip nonlinear unit (NOFU) that avoids bulky amplifiers, high-fidelity Clements-style interferometer meshes for matrix multiplication, and a scalable training method using simultaneous perturbation that requires only two forward passes per update. The chip achieves digital-level accuracy (92.7%) on a vowel classification task while operating at अत्यंत high speeds (~435 ps latency), demonstrating that optical neural networks can be both practical and competitive. Although current energy use is limited by inefficient thermal components, the work provides a clear path toward massively faster and more energy-efficient systems, marking a major step from theory to real-world photonic AI hardware.
Paper 9 — Feldmann et al. (2021), "Parallel convolutional processing using an integrated photonic tensor core"
The Feldmann et al. 2021 photonic tensor core convolution paper demonstrates the first photonic chip capable of performing parallel convolution operations by combining three key technologies: a soliton microcomb that generates hundreds of wavelengths to carry multiple input vectors simultaneously, phase-change material (PCM) cells that store weights non-volatilely as an optical in-memory matrix, and wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) to process multiple matrix-vector multiplications in parallel through the same hardware. This enables true hardware reuse and massive parallelism without duplicating circuitry, achieving real ML performance (95.3% MNIST accuracy close to software) while operating at GHz speeds and tera-MAC throughput. Unlike prior optical or electronic systems, computation here occurs passively as light propagates through the matrix, with scalability driven by adding wavelengths rather than hardware. Overall, it establishes a new paradigm for high-density, energy-efficient AI hardware, showing that integrated photonics can rival electronics while offering a path to petascale performance.
